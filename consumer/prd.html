Section 0
What This Platform Is — and Is Not
The structure this platform is built for
One demand party wants enrichment on their existing user base. That user base may be B2B or B2C. Multiple supply parties hold data on those users that is relevant to the enrichment query. The output is not just raw enrichment — it also includes computation of derived metrics. A supply party does not simply hand over a field value; they compute a result. For example: PayU matches against Swiggy's user base to determine share of wallet for Swiggy users — not just whether a user exists in PayU's database, but a computed signal derived from their transaction data.
Matching between the demand party's user list and the supply party's database happens either via direct identifier match (phone number, email) or fuzzy match on a shared attribute. Neither side sees the other's raw data at any point.
Why this is a standalone platform — not a unified one across enrichment use cases
SL's MPC infrastructure is applicable to multiple use cases beyond data enrichment — AML screening, freight matching, and others. A reasonable question is whether all of these should live in one platform. They should not, and the reason is straightforward: the buyer is not the same.
Use caseBuyerWho owns the UI
Data enrichment Product or data team at a fintech, lender, or super-app. Cares about match rate, signal quality, and speed of enrichment. SL — there is no existing player to hand the interface to. This is the exception.
AML screening Compliance officer or risk team, likely at a bank or regulated financial institution. Entirely different procurement cycle, regulatory requirements, and success criteria. The existing player — a bank's compliance system. SL is infrastructure underneath.
Freight matching Logistics operator or freight platform. Different vocabulary, different infrastructure expectations, different commercial model. The existing player — the freight platform. SL is infrastructure underneath.
A single platform optimised for one of these buyers would actively feel wrong to the others. Designing for all three would mean designing well for none. Data enrichment is, for now, the only use case where SL owns the product surface directly — which is precisely what makes it the right candidate for a standalone platform.
The Databricks question
Databricks has a unified platform spanning a data lakehouse, clean rooms, dashboards, and apps — multiple products, one surface. If they can unify, why can't SL?
Databricks can unify because all of their products share three things: one underlying primitive (the lakehouse — every product is a new way to use data that already lives there), one buyer persona (data engineers and data scientists, regardless of the tool), and one product surface (customers log into Databricks directly; no intermediary owns the UI). Adding a new Databricks product requires no new infrastructure contract and no new deployment model. The user is already there.
Across SL's enrichment use cases, none of these three conditions hold. The buyer is different. The deployment context is different. And in AML and freight matching, SL is not the product surface — an existing operator is. Unification would create a platform that fits no buyer cleanly.
Flexibility for the future
This is a deliberate decision for now — not a permanent constraint. If the market signals that buyers are converging, or that a unified surface creates a genuine advantage, the platform should be able to accommodate that. It is important that the architecture is built with enough flexibility that unification is a future option, not an impossibility. The decision today is to not unify prematurely.
Section 1
Why We're Building This
This platform is the product delivery layer for SL's independent data enrichment use case. It exists specifically where Silence Laboratories is connecting a demand partner (a lender, a fintech, a super-app) with a supply partner (a bank, a telecom operator, a payment aggregator) — neither of whom would share raw data with the other directly.
Illustrative pairings: Grab × telecom operator, PayU × Swiggy, a digital lender × HDFC Data Services. SL is the neutral infrastructure that makes the computation possible without either side seeing the other's data.
This is not a general-purpose MPC platform. It is scoped to: one or more demand partners matched with one or more supply partners, with pre-agreed enrichment queries, triggered via a product interface, with billing attached. The platform replaces a manual, email-driven coordination process.
Section 2

Once demand and supply partners are commercially onboarded and agreements are signed, they currently have no self-serve interface to act on those agreements. The coordination to actually run a computation — selecting enrichments, sharing data, receiving outputs — is manual and slow.

Section 4
Architecture — Node Setup and Computation Model
This section describes the infrastructure model underlying every enrichment computation. Understanding it is a prerequisite for node provisioning, SDK deployment, and reasoning about multi-party scale.
Recommended approach: Bilateral multi-party computation (Option A).

Node setup and SDK installation happen once, offline, when a partner onboards. After that, the platform is fully self-serve — demand partners can initiate computations against any active supply partner without any engineering involvement. Adding a new supply partner means one new node provisioned at onboarding; no existing nodes or sharings change.

This model is validated by comparable platforms (e.g. ad targeting data platforms) that follow the same pattern: heavy offline onboarding once, then self-serve computation on top. Multi-party MPC (Option B) introduces additional complexity depending on the deployment model chosen. Three sub-options are documented below: a fixed 3-server model hosted by SL or a trusted third party (B1), a party-hosted node model with resharding implications (B2), and a homomorphic encryption model with no sharding (B3 — future roadmap). All three are documented below for reference.
Option A — Bilateral Model
Each computation is an independent 2-party protocol. Every party provisions their own persistent node once. The platform runs computations as bilateral pairs — A×B, A×C — and combines results at the end. Adding a new supply partner means adding a new node; no existing party re-provisions.

Can A×B and A×C run in parallel?
Yes — they are independent bilateral computations with no shared state between B and C. Node A handles them as isolated execution contexts. The only constraint is Node A's compute capacity. If A×B and A×C must run sequentially due to resource limits, SL's orchestration node handles queuing; results are combined at the end either way. Supply partners B and C are never aware of each other.
🔵 Open design question
Concurrent computation capacity on Node A: What is the maximum number of simultaneous bilateral computations Node A can support? This determines whether multi-supply runs are truly parallel or queued sequentially. Needs input from SL Engineering before specifying a throughput SLA for demand partners.
🔵 Open design question
What does "isolated context" mean inside a single node?

When Node A runs A×B and A×C simultaneously, it holds different transient data shares per context. The node software must enforce strict separation so no data bleeds between them. Two options:

Logical isolation: Node A runs multiple contexts as isolated processes or threads within one node instance. No new hardware per run, but requires strong software-level isolation guarantees.

One instance per bilateral pair: Each A×B, A×C run gets its own node instance — warm standby per supply partner, or spun fresh per run. Stronger isolation by design, but a supply partner with many demand partners needs multiple instances running concurrently.

This affects node provisioning, infrastructure specs, and SLA. Needs resolution from SL Engineering.
Option B — Multi-Party Computation (MPC)
MPC allows more than two parties to jointly compute a function without any party revealing their private input. Unlike Option A (bilateral pairs), all participating parties are involved in a single protocol. However, the deployment model for the computation nodes is a key architectural decision — and it has significant implications for operations, trust, and scalability. Three sub-options are described below.
B1 — Fixed 3-server model (SL-hosted or trusted third party)
Computation always runs across exactly 3 servers, regardless of how many data partners participate. These servers are not hosted by the data partners — they are operated by SL or a designated trusted third party. Partners contribute their data (secret-shared across the 3 servers) at onboarding, but do not provision or manage nodes themselves.
Server 1 — SL / Third Party
· Share 1 of all partners' data
· Hosted and managed by SL
Server 2 — SL / Third Party
· Share 2 of all partners' data
· Hosted and managed by SL
Server 3 — SL / Third Party
· Share 3 of all partners' data
· Hosted and managed by SL
Data partners do not run nodes. Adding a new partner means re-sharing their data to the same 3 servers — no new infrastructure required.
Key implication: Partners must trust SL (or the third party) to honestly operate the servers. This is operationally simpler for partners but places infrastructure responsibility — and reputational risk — on SL. Security guarantees depend on the servers being non-colluding.
B2 — Each party hosts their own node
Every data partner provisions and operates their own MPC node. Data is secret-shared across all active nodes. Computation runs as a joint protocol across all party nodes simultaneously. Partners retain full infrastructure control.
Node A — Demand Partner
· A₁ — own data
· B₁ — share of B's data
· C₁ — share of C's data
Node B — Supply Partner 1
· A₂ — share of A's data
· B₂ — own data
· C₂ — share of C's data
Node C — Supply Partner 2
· A₃ — share of A's data
· B₃ — share of B's data
· C₃ — own data
All nodes participate jointly. When a new partner D joins, all parties must re-shard their data into N+1 shares and re-provision.
Key implication: Every time a new partner joins, all existing parties must re-shard their data and redistribute shares to include the new node. This is a significant operational event — it is not self-serve, and it scales poorly in a dynamic marketplace where partners join incrementally.
B3 — Each party holds their own data, no sharding (Homomorphic Encryption) — Future roadmap
Each party keeps their data on their own node, fully encrypted. There is no secret sharing or data distribution. Computation happens directly on encrypted data between relevant parties using homomorphic encryption (HE). A new partner joining requires no changes to any existing party's node or data.
Node A — Demand Partner
· Full dataset A, encrypted locally
· No shares sent to other parties
Node B — Supply Partner 1
· Full dataset B, encrypted locally
· No shares sent to other parties
Node C — Supply Partner 2
· Full dataset C, encrypted locally
· No shares sent to other parties
Computation runs on ciphertexts. Each party's plaintext data never leaves their node. New partners can join without any resharing event.
Not ready for v1.0. HE is computationally expensive, especially for complex or multi-variable functions. Current performance makes it impractical for production workloads at the enrichment scale this platform targets. This option belongs on the roadmap — not the near-term architecture. It should be revisited as HE tooling matures.

Section 5
Protocols Supported — Enrichment Function Model
Every enrichment computation runs as a cryptographic protocol over the matched user set. The question this section addresses is: how are those protocols defined, and who can define them? There are two architecturally different approaches.
Latency baseline: Enrichment computation does not need to be real-time. A latency of minutes is acceptable — this meaningfully expands what is technically feasible for both options below. The goal is correctness and privacy, not sub-second response.
Option 1 — Function library
Enrichment functions are built one at a time as use cases arrive. Each function — income threshold, subscriber status, spend band, EMI detection — is a purpose-built MPC protocol, designed, tested, and deployed by SL Engineering. Once built, it is available to any demand partner whose DSA covers it.
DimensionDetail
How functions are added Demand or supply partner identifies a need. SL Engineering scopes and builds the protocol. It enters the enrichment catalogue once validated.
Coverage today Most common enrichment types — threshold comparisons, categorical lookups, band aggregations — are likely already built or close. The long tail of niche queries is the gap.
Efficiency Each protocol is purpose-optimised. Good efficiency per function, but adding new functions has engineering lead time — not self-serve.
Demand partner experience Selects from a catalogue of available enrichment types. Cannot define custom logic. If a needed function isn't in the catalogue, they raise a request and wait for SL Engineering to build it.
Option 2 — Flexible computation layer
A translation layer sits between the demand partner's query and the MPC protocol. The demand partner specifies what they want — a field, an operator, a threshold, an output format — and the platform translates this into a valid MPC protocol at runtime. Any expressible query can run without SL Engineering involvement per function.
DimensionDetail
How functions are added Demand partner defines the query directly on the platform — field name, condition, output type. The translation layer generates the protocol. No per-function engineering work by SL.
Coverage Theoretically unlimited — any query the translation layer can parse and validate. In practice, bounded by what the translation layer supports (field types, operators, aggregation methods).
Efficiency Generalised protocols are less efficient than purpose-built ones. Given that latency of minutes is acceptable, this may be a tolerable tradeoff — but it needs validation with SL Engineering on what the floor is.
Demand partner experience True self-serve for enrichment definition. Partner defines what they want; platform handles translation and validation. Significantly higher product complexity to build and maintain.
Risk The translation layer itself is a significant engineering challenge. Incorrectly translated protocols could produce wrong results or, worse, leak information. Protocol validation before execution is a hard requirement.
Tradeoff and recommendation
Option 1 — Function libraryOption 2 — Flexible layer
Time to first enrichmentFast — if function already existsSlower — translation layer must be built first
Adding a new enrichment typeSL Engineering builds it — days to weeksDemand partner defines it — minutes
Protocol efficiencyHigh — purpose-optimised per functionLower — generalised; acceptable if minutes of latency is the floor
Build complexityLow per function; grows linearlyHigh upfront; scales well once built
Protocol correctness guaranteeTested per function before deploymentRequires runtime validation — harder to guarantee
🔵 Open design question
Recommended path: Option 1 now, Option 2 as a strategic investment.

Start with a function library. Most enrichment use cases across the first wave of partners — income thresholds, subscriber status, spend bands, EMI flags — can be covered with a small, well-curated set of purpose-built protocols. Build these well, get them into production, and observe what demand partners actually ask for.

Option 2 becomes the right investment when: (a) the function request queue from demand partners is high and the per-function engineering cost is a bottleneck, or (b) a demand partner class emerges that needs truly custom computation logic that cannot be approximated by catalogue functions.

The decision point is not architectural — it is commercial. When does the catalogue start saying no too often?

Section 6
Onboarding — Handholding to Self-Serve
The offline setup steps in Section 5 are currently handled entirely by SL Engineering and Solutions. To scale beyond 3–5 partners, some of these steps will need to move onto the platform. The strategy is deliberate: handhold the first cohort completely, learn what breaks, then build self-serve tooling for the steps that are high-friction or error-prone.
Phase strategy: First 1–3 partners onboarded fully by SL Engineering — white-glove, offline. Use that experience to identify which steps are highest friction, most error-prone, or most frequently asked about. Build platform tooling for those steps first. Do not build self-serve infrastructure speculatively.

That audit should directly drive what gets built for self-serve in v2. No self-serve build decision should be made before that data exists.

Phase 2 — Online
Self-serve on the platform
Demand Partner Online Flow
1
Log in → go to Marketplace
Demand partner logs in with credentials issued by SL. Lands on Marketplace, which shows all supply partners and their agreement status — Active, Pending, or Not Enabled.
2
Select one or more data providers
Only providers with an Active DSA can be selected. Pending and Not Enabled providers are visible but locked. The demand partner selects the providers they want to enrich against in this run.
3
Choose enrichment types per provider
For each selected provider, the demand partner picks which enrichment signals they want — e.g. income band, recharge pattern, EMI status. Only enrichments covered under their DSA are shown.
4
Upload user data
Uploads a CSV with the agreed identifier column (phone number, email, or custom ID). The same file is used for matching against all selected providers. The raw data stays on the demand partner's infrastructure — only encrypted, secret-shared representations are transmitted to the SL compute nodes.
5
Review configuration and initiate computation
Reviews a summary of providers, enrichments selected, and data source. Confirms and triggers the run. From this point, computation is handled by the SL infrastructure.
6
Computation enters pending state — awaiting supply partner approval
Once the demand partner initiates, the run is queued and enters a pending state. Each supply partner in the run must manually approve before PSI can begin. Manual approval is required for every computation request — there is no auto-approve even when an active DSA is in place. Notification UX for supply partner approval is a separate design decision.
7
PSI runs — per provider
Private Set Intersection runs separately against each provider's database. Hashed identifiers from both sides are compared. Only the intersection count is revealed — neither side sees the other's full list or which specific users matched.
8
MPC runs — enrichment computation on matched set
For each provider, enrichment computation runs only on the users who matched in PSI. The supply partner computes the agreed signals on their data. The demand partner's Customer IDs are never shared with the supply partner at any point.
9
Output delivered — available for download
Results appear in the platform: one row per matched user, identified by the demand partner's own Customer ID, with one column per enrichment per provider. Output is downloadable as CSV. An audit certificate is also available for compliance records.
Key constraint: Steps 1–5 are blocked until offline setup (Phase 1) is complete. Steps 6–9 are blocked until the demand partner has an Active DSA with at least one supply partner. The platform enforces both gates — it does not manage them.
Section 9
Supply Partner — End-to-End Experience
The supply partner journey also spans an offline setup phase and an online response phase. Unlike the demand partner, the supply partner does not initiate computation — they receive requests, approve them, and the platform handles the rest. Their view of the output is intentionally minimal.
Phase 1 — Offline
Managed by SL before platform access is granted
1
Commercial agreement with Silence Labs signed
Supply partner signs commercial terms covering data monetisation, permissible use, and liability. SL Sales owns this.
2
DSA finalised with each demand partner
A separate Data Sharing Agreement is required for each demand partner the supply partner will serve. The DSA defines which enrichments are permitted, pricing per record, and permissible use. SL Legal facilitates.
3
Enrichment functions and computations aligned
SL Solutions works with the supply partner to define the exact computation logic: field names, threshold values, output format. Examples: income > ₹10,000 → Yes/No, subscriber status → Pre-paid / Post-paid, avg monthly recharge → band of ₹0–200 / ₹200–500 / >₹500. This determines what the platform's enrichment catalogue shows for that supply partner.
4
Node provisioned and SDK installed
SL Engineering spins up a secure compute node on the supply partner's infrastructure and installs the Silent Compute SDK. This is where PSI and MPC will run — the supply partner's data never leaves their environment.
5
Platform credentials issued
SL Admin creates the supply partner account. Agreement flags for each active demand partner are activated at this point. The supply partner can log in and see their enrichment catalogue and incoming requests.
Phase 2 — Online
Reactive — supply partner responds to incoming requests
Supply Partner Online Flow
1
Computation request received from a demand partner
When a demand partner initiates a run, the supply partner sees a pending request on their dashboard — which demand partner, which enrichments, estimated record count. No demand partner user data is visible at this stage.

3
Data made available on the node
The supply partner's data must be present on their secure node before PSI can run.
🔵 Open design question
Is the supply partner's data pre-loaded on the node, or pushed fresh per run?

Option A — Pre-loaded: Supply partner's database is synced to the node on a schedule (daily / weekly). PSI runs against this snapshot. Simpler UX — no action needed per run. Raises questions about data freshness and storage.

Option B — Per-run push: Supply partner actively uploads or pushes a fresh dataset for each computation request. More control and freshness, but adds friction and an extra step to every run.

This decision affects the supply partner's online flow significantly and should be resolved before build.
4

5
MPC runs — enrichment computation on matched set

Run completes — supply partner sees a summary only
The output is delivered directly to the demand partner. The supply partner does not see the enriched output or the demand partner's user data. Their view is limited to: which demand partner the run was for, which computation types ran, how many records were matched, and the earnings generated for this run.

Section 10
Costs and Pricing
Three distinct cost and pricing layers sit underneath every computation. Each is owned differently and set through a different process.
Cost layerWho bears itHow it's set
Node infrastructure Each partner bears their own — demand partner pays for Node A, supply partner pays for Node B. SL pays for the Orchestration node. Partners provision and run nodes on their own infrastructure. SL Engineering assists setup but does not subsidise running costs.
Computation pricing (per record / per run) Demand partner pays supply partner for access to enrichment data Defined offline in the DSA. SL is in the loop on every negotiation — SL quotes, facilitates, and the rate is formalised in the agreement before the platform is activated for that pair.
SL platform fee Taken from the computation pricing flow SL's margin is built into the pricing it negotiates — not a separate line item visible to partners.
Payment collection flow
Once a computation run completes, the demand partner owes the supply partner for the matched and enriched records. There are two ways this payment can flow:
🔵 Open design question
Option 1 — Via SL (preferred): Demand partner pays SL. SL takes its margin and remits the supply partner's share. SL controls the billing relationship with both sides, has full visibility into revenue, and can enforce payment before releasing results.

Option 2 — Direct to supply partner: Demand partner pays the supply partner directly. Supply partner then remits SL's fee separately. Simpler for the partners but SL loses visibility and control — SL's fee collection depends on the supply partner initiating a separate transfer.

Option 1 is preferred. It keeps SL in the billing relationship on both sides, makes revenue reconciliation straightforward, and gives SL a natural enforcement mechanism — results are not released until payment is confirmed.
Not on the platform in v1: Billing on the platform shows a read-only view of usage and spend. Actual payment processing, invoicing, and remittance are handled offline in v1. The platform records what ran and how many records were matched; the money movement happens outside it.
Rate card and margin mechanics
For pricing to scale — and for SL to quote consistently across deals — supply partner rates need to be stored centrally and versioned. SL adds a margin layer on top before quoting to demand partners. Neither side sees the other's number.
LayerWhat it isWho sets itVisible to partners?
Supply partner base rate Price per matched record, per enrichment type, set by the supply partner SL negotiates with supply partner offline. Stored in SL's internal system. Not visible to demand partners
SL margin Percentage or fixed markup applied on top of base rate SL internal — configurable per supply partner or per enrichment type. Can be updated without renegotiating DSAs. Not visible to either partner
Demand partner price Base rate + SL margin = the rate quoted to and agreed by the demand partner in their DSA SL quotes this to demand partner. Formalised in the DSA. Visible to demand partner (their own rate only)
Ideal target state: SL maintains a standard rate card per supply partner — updated periodically as relationships mature or volumes change. When a new demand partner is onboarded, SL pulls the current base rate, applies the margin, and quotes. No custom pricing per deal unless the demand partner is large enough to warrant it. This keeps quoting fast and margin consistent.
🔵 Open question
Where is the rate card stored? Today this likely lives in a spreadsheet or in the DSA documents. For quoting and billing to be consistent at scale, it needs to be in a system SL can update centrally — a simple internal admin tool or a structured config file at minimum. This does not need to be partner-facing, but it needs to be auditable and versionable. Should be scoped before the third partner is onboarded.

What We're Building — Platform Views and Build Scope
The platform has three distinct views, each built for a different user. Two are external-facing. One is internal only.

Demand partner view (external)
The interface a lender, fintech, or super-app logs into. This is the primary product surface — the view through which enrichment runs are initiated, results are downloaded, and billing is tracked. Key screens: marketplace, enrichment selection, data upload, computation status, reports, and billing.
Supply partner view (external)
The interface a bank, telecom, or data provider logs into. Reactive by design — the supply partner does not initiate computation, they respond to it. Key screens: incoming computation requests (approve / reject), enrichment catalogue, node status, and earnings.
Admin view (internal — SL only)
An internal configuration interface used by SL to manage the platform on behalf of partners. Not visible to demand or supply partners. Organised into two tabs — supply partners and demand partners — so SL can manage each side independently and see the full state of the network at a glance.
ModuleDetail
Supply partner tab List of all supply partners and their onboarding status (not started / in progress / onboarded). Per partner: toggle enablement flag (active / inactive), configure base rates per enrichment type, view which demand partners have active computation agreements.
Demand partner tab List of all demand partners and their onboarding status. Per partner: toggle enablement flag (active / inactive), view active supply agreements, enable or disable specific computation types per demand-supply pairing — reflecting what has been agreed in the DSA.
Pricing and cost configuration Set and update supply partner base rate per enrichment type. Set and update SL margin (percentage or fixed) per supply partner or enrichment type. View effective demand partner price (base rate + margin) without either partner seeing the other's number. Rate card versioned and auditable — changes logged with date.
Internal sign-in Separate authentication from the partner-facing login. SL team members only. Role-based access within the admin panel is a future consideration — in v1, all SL admin users have full access.
Key principle: The admin panel is the only place where partner enablement flags and computation agreements are set. The partner-facing views enforce these flags but do not manage them. All configuration is SL-owned and SL-controlled.
