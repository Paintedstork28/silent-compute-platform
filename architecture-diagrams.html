Silent Compute — Architecture Models
Two ways to set up the computation network. Different trust models, different network effects, different operational implications.
Model 1
2PC — Bilateral, Party-Hosted Nodes
Each party runs their own node in their own infrastructure. Computation happens as a series of independent bilateral pairs. Data never leaves anyone's environment. Every new relationship requires a separate setup.

Model 2
MPC — 3-Server Network, Shared Infrastructure
Three independent servers sit at the centre, operated by SL or a trusted third party. Every party shards their data to all three servers at onboarding. Once on the network, any party can compute against any other — no bilateral setup required per relationship.

Side-by-Side Comparison
The same question answered differently by each model.
2PC — Bilateral MPC — 3-Server
Who hosts compute nodes Each party in their own infrastructure SL or a trusted third party (always 3 fixed servers)
Where data lives Entirely within each party's own environment. Never leaves. Sharded across the 3 servers. No single server holds complete data.
Adding a new party New party sets up a node. Must establish bilateral relationship with each counterpart separately. O(N) new setups. New party shards data to the existing 3 servers. Instantly computable with everyone already on the network. O(1) setup.
Network effect None automatic. Every relationship requires its own commercial and technical setup. Strong. Every new party makes the network more valuable for everyone already on it.
Trust required Parties only need to trust their bilateral counterpart and SL's orchestration layer. All parties must trust that at least 2 of 3 servers are honest and non-colluding.
Regulatory fit High. Data never leaves the party's own infrastructure. Easier for regulated entities (banks, telecoms). Harder. Sharding data to SL's servers requires regulatory sign-off for most banks and telecoms.
Operational complexity Each party must provision, maintain, and monitor their own node. Parties have no infrastructure to manage. SL bears the operational burden.
Right for Regulated supply partners who cannot send data outside their environment. Early-stage where bilateral DSAs are manageable. When the network is large enough that bilateral setups become a bottleneck, and trust in SL's infrastructure is established.
